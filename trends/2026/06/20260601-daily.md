## Pick Up

1. [Malicious npm packages detected across Red Hat Cloud Services](https://github.com/RedHatInsights/javascript-clients/issues/492)
   - Why I recommend: A live supply chain attack hit Red Hat's `javascript-clients` monorepo — this is exactly the kind of high-signal security incident ML engineers need to watch because it shows how typosquatting and compromised CI pipelines can reach production cloud services at scale.

2. [Miasma: Supply Chain Attack Targeting RedHat npm Packages](https://www.wiz.io/blog/miasma-supply-chain-attack-targeting-redhat-npm-packages)
   - Why I recommend: Wiz's technical deep-dive on the same Red Hat npm incident above — read this alongside the GitHub issue for attacker TTPs, indicators of compromise, and detection strategies that translate directly to defending ML/data pipelines.

3. [A 10 year old Xeon is all you need](https://point.free/blog/gemma-4-on-a-2016-xeon/)
   - Why I recommend: Running Gemma 4 on a 2016 Xeon server challenges the "you need expensive GPUs" assumption — a practical data point for ML engineers evaluating inference cost/performance tradeoffs with modern open-weight models.

4. [Anthropic confidentially submits draft S-1 to the SEC](https://www.anthropic.com/news/confidential-draft-s1-sec)
   - Why I recommend: Anthropic going public will reshape the competitive landscape for AI labs, foundation model pricing, and enterprise AI adoption — important strategic context for anyone working in or building on LLMs.

5. [CS336: Language Modeling from Scratch](https://cs336.stanford.edu/)
   - Why I recommend: Stanford's course on building language models from scratch is a rare structured curriculum that covers implementation details most practitioners lack; useful as a self-study roadmap or to understand what strong new ML graduates know.

6. [AI Agent Guidelines for CS336 at Stanford](https://github.com/stanford-cs336/assignment1-basics/blob/main/CLAUDE.md)
   - Why I recommend: Stanford's CLAUDE.md for their LM course defines how students should use AI agents in assignments — this framing of "AI as a tool with guardrails" is a practical template for teams setting internal AI coding policies.

7. [The newest Instagram "exploit" is the goofiest I've seen](https://www.0xsid.com/blog/meta-account-takeover-fiasco)
   - Why I recommend: Highest-engagement security story of the day (1040 pts); a creative account takeover chain involving Meta's infrastructure that illustrates how OAuth and account-linking flows can be abused in subtle ways.

8. [Florida sues OpenAI and Sam Altman over AI risks](https://www.politico.com/news/2026/06/01/openai-hit-with-florida-lawsuit-00944215)
   - Why I recommend: State-level AI litigation is accelerating — understanding the legal framing of AI risk (consumer protection vs. product liability) matters for ML engineers building commercial systems.

9. [State of Post Quantum Cryptography](https://www.wiz.io/blog/state-of-post-quantum-cryptography)
   - Why I recommend: Wiz's survey of PQC adoption across cloud environments gives a concrete picture of where enterprise TLS/key management stands relative to NIST standards — relevant for anyone building secure ML infrastructure.

10. [State of SDLC Security 2026: How Risk Scales in Modern Development](https://www.wiz.io/blog/sdlc-security-report-2026-key-takeaways)
    - Why I recommend: Data-driven report on where code-to-cloud vulnerabilities actually originate in 2026 — useful for ML teams thinking about supply chain and dependency risk in training and serving pipelines.
